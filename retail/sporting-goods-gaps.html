Where Sporting Goods Brands Are Losing — and Where the White Space Is

A competitive gap analysis across 18 brands: heritage sport, premium performance, outdoor, digital-native and value players. Product coverage, DTC maturity, omni-channel capability, European footprint, and 9 white space opportunities. Updated Q1 2026.

Updated Q1 2026 18 brands · 11 capability dimensions Scope: Global strategy + European retail presence Sources: Company annual reports, Wikipedia, brand websites — editorial analysis
Editorial note: The gap matrix, capability assessments, and omni-channel scoring in this report are editorial analysis based on publicly observable brand capabilities. They are not survey data. The macro statistics in the opening (−8% spend, +22% outdoor growth, +34% resale growth, 61% value-seeking shoppers) are directional editorial observations — not from a named public source — and should be treated as illustrative context, not verified data. DTC percentages are estimates; see methodology for detail.
European consumer spending on non-essential sporting goods came under pressure in 2024 as cost-of-living pressures squeezed discretionary budgets. At the same time, Nike and Adidas faced revenue pressure — not primarily from each other, but from challenger brands filling specific unmet needs. Lululemon built a $10.6B business on community and DTC (FY2024 · Wikipedia). Gymshark reached £556M revenue in 2023 (Wikipedia) with a primarily digital model. On Running grew from CHF ~800M (2021) to CHF 2.32B (2024) — roughly 3× in three years (Wikipedia). Meanwhile, the mass of heritage brands — Champion, Reebok, Fila, Puma — compete on the same axes: logo, price promotion, and athlete licensing. The window to claim unoccupied territory is open. It will not stay open.

The DTC divide is structural, not cyclical. Gymshark and Lululemon were born DTC — they have no wholesale dependency to unwind. Nike and Adidas have been actively reducing wholesale since 2021 (publicly confirmed in annual reports). On Running built its CHF 2.32B business (2024, Wikipedia) predominantly direct. Champion is clearly wholesale-dominant — heavily reliant on partners (Zalando, SportsDirect, department stores) who control the customer relationship, pricing, and data.

Champion owns Athleisure and Collegiate outright — the only two categories where no competitor scores higher. The fast-growing categories (Outdoor/Trail, Running) are dominated by Salomon, Arc’teryx, On Running, and ASICS at prices 3–5× Champion’s range. Decathlon is the only brand with solid presence across all 8 categories — breadth is Decathlon’s moat. Nobody else competes on breadth; they compete on depth in chosen categories. Champion needs a category depth strategy, not a category expansion strategy.

Champion’s European footprint is thin. Strong in France (Hanesbrands’ heritage market) and Southern Europe (Italy, Spain), but no brand stores in the Netherlands, absent in Scandinavia, and limited in Germany — Europe’s largest economy. Lululemon, with a fraction of Champion’s history, already operates in more European markets. On Running, founded in 2010, has brand presence in 9 key European cities. Champion’s European distribution strategy relies heavily on wholesale partners rather than brand-controlled environments, which limits brand experience, pricing power, and customer data.

Champion operates approximately 8 brand stores in Europe — fewer than Gymshark, a 10-year-old digital brand that opened its first physical store in 2022. Adidas (82 stores) and The North Face (54 stores) use their European retail estate to control brand narrative, train sales staff, and capture first-party data. Each Champion wholesale transaction hands that opportunity to a third party. The case for a selective Champion flagship strategy in 3–5 European capitals (London, Paris, Amsterdam, Berlin, Milan) is commercially strong and strategically urgent.

Champion scores 0 out of 6 omni-channel criteria. This makes it one of only two brands in this analysis (alongside Fila) with no unified commerce capability. Nike shows real-time store stock across all locations. Lululemon store staff can access your online wishlist and purchase history. Every capability that Champion lacks hands value — customer data, pricing control, repeat engagement — to wholesale intermediaries like Zalando and Sports Direct. With just 8 stores, building omni-channel infrastructure is a focused, achievable investment — not the enterprise transformation it represents for a brand with hundreds of locations.

The Macro Environment — Why the Window Is Open Now

European consumer spending on non-essential categories has been under sustained pressure since 2022, driven by energy price rises, inflation, and declining real wages. Sporting goods — particularly mid-tier apparel and footwear — sit at the intersection of essential (exercise, wellbeing) and discretionary (fashion, brand aspiration). This dual position makes the category resilient in volume but sensitive to price tier.

Cost-of-living pressure has demonstrably accelerated two structural shifts: first, a move toward value-positioned brands and own-label (Decathlon, Primark Sport); second, a growth in secondhand and circular purchasing — platforms like Vinted, Back Market, and branded trade-in schemes have all reported growth in sport/outdoor categories since 2022.

Simultaneously, the premium end has grown. On Running (CHF 2.32B revenue 2024, up ~3× from 2021 — Wikipedia), Lululemon ($10.6B FY2024 — Wikipedia), and Arc'teryx have demonstrated that premium-priced, community-positioned brands can grow rapidly even in a squeezed consumer environment. This bifurcation — value and premium both growing, mid-market squeezed — is the structural context for Champion's positioning challenge.

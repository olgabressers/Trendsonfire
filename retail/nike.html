Nike: The Transformation That Had to Happen
— and What Comes Next

Nike's 2022–2026 reinvention: the DTC gamble, revenue decline, market share loss to On Running and HOKA, the CEO change to Elliott Hill, and the long road to recovery. Covering EMEA operations, competitive landscape, and structural challenges ahead.

Nike’s FY2025 confirmed the scale of the challenge: revenue fell 10% to $46.3B, net income dropped 44% to $3.2B, and Nike Direct declined 13% (Nike IR, FY2025 annual results). The DTC-first strategy under John Donahoe — which saw Nike systematically cut wholesale partners from 2017 onward — underperformed expectations, particularly in EMEA. On Running (CHF 2.32B revenue FY2024, up ~3× from 2021 — Wikipedia) and HOKA filled the performance running gap. Lululemon ($10.6B FY2024 — Wikipedia) captured women’s. The return of Elliott Hill as CEO in October 2024 signalled a fundamental reset: back to sport, back to wholesale, back to innovation. FY2026 Q1–Q2 show early stabilisation — revenue roughly flat YoY — but profitability remains under pressure with gross margins down 300+ basis points. The transformation is far from over.

Transformation Timeline — 2022 to 2026
How Nike moved from bold DTC pioneer to restructuring mode — and what the recovery looks like.
2022
2022 — Peak DTC Confidence
Wholesale Reduction Accelerates — 50% of Retail Partners Dropped Since 2017
Under Donahoe, Nike continues cutting wholesale accounts — Zappos, DSW, Urban Outfitters, and hundreds of regional independents are gone. The thesis: own the consumer relationship directly through Nike.com, SNKRS, and NRC/NTC apps. DTC share hits ~42%. Inventory build-up begins in distribution centres as demand softens post-Covid.
2023
2023 — The Cracks Appear
Inventory Crisis, Margin Pressure, and the First Signs of Share Loss
Nike carries $9.7B of inventory — a record — forcing heavy promotions and discounting that damages the brand's premium positioning. Meanwhile, On Running and HOKA grow 50%+ year-on-year in EMEA running. Nike's running market share falls below 50% for the first time in 15 years. The company's response is too slow; product pipelines are locked 18 months ahead.
Feb 24
February 2024 — Restructuring
1,600 Layoffs Announced — Part of the $2B Cost-Saving Programme
Nike announces a workforce reduction of ~1,600 jobs (~2% of global headcount), following a December 2023 announcement of a $2B cost-saving programme over three years. The cuts target corporate and headquarters roles, not stores or distribution. Source: CNN Business (Feb 2024) · OPB.
Oct 24
October 2024 — CEO Reset
Elliott Hill Returns — End of the Donahoe Era
John Donahoe steps down; Elliott Hill, a long-tenured Nike veteran, returns from retirement to lead the recovery effective 14 October 2024. His mandate: restore sport credibility, rebuild wholesale partnerships, re-engage with athletes and retail partners, and accelerate product innovation. Source: Nike Investor Relations announcement.
2025
2025 — The Rebuild
"Win Now" Strategy — Wholesale Re-engagement, Product Offence, EMEA Prioritised
Hill launches recovery strategy: wholesale partnerships actively rebuilt (Foot Locker, JD Sports, Intersport re-engaged), new product franchises in running and training. FY2025 full-year results confirm the depth of the reset: revenue $46.3B (down 10%), net income $3.2B (down 44%), Nike Direct down 13%, digital down 20% (Nike IR). EMEA revenue falls to $12.3B from $13.6B. Wholesale revenue down 7% but showing signs of stabilisation by Q4.
2026
FY2026 — Early Stabilisation, Margins Still Under Pressure
Revenue Flat YoY — But Profitability Remains Challenged
FY2026 Q1 (Aug 2025): revenue $11.7B, up 1% reported but down 1% currency-neutral. Wholesale up 7% — the clearest sign that partner re-engagement is working. But Nike Direct down 4%, digital down 12%, and gross margin down 320 basis points (Nike IR). FY2026 Q2 (Nov 2025): revenue $12.4B, up 1%. EMEA down 1%, with growth in CEE/Middle East offset by slight Western Europe decline. Net income still declining 31% YoY in Q1. The revenue line is stabilising; profitability recovery has not yet begun.

8 High-Priority Opportunities for Nike EMEA
Where Nike has the assets, brand equity, and operational base to reclaim leadership — with the right transformation capability behind each.
Opportunity 01
Reclaim Running — Before On and HOKA Make It Permanent
Nike invented modern running culture. It lost it to On and HOKA while managing inventory and pushing DTC. The Vomero 18, Pegasus 41, and Alphafly franchise are strong foundations — but the product pipeline and the retail placement strategy need an urgent rethink for EMEA. The window is still open: neither On nor HOKA has Nike's distribution or brand scale.
Scope: Product strategy, commercial planning, category management
Opportunity 02
Women's Category — Build What Lululemon Can't: Performance at Scale
Lululemon owns women's activewear at premium. Nike has the performance credentials, the athlete roster (Serena, Caster, Dina Asher-Smith), and the supply chain to dominate women's sport-to-street. But the brand investment, product design, and community strategy haven't matched the opportunity. An EMEA-specific women's category plan — product, marketing, and DTC — is overdue.
Scope: Category strategy, brand planning, DTC operations
Opportunity 03
Wholesale Excellence — Turn Rebuilt Partnerships Into Competitive Advantage
The wholesale reversal under Hill is the right call. But re-engagement is not enough — Nike needs to build structured joint business planning, category-level sell-through analytics, and a partner tiering model that makes top accounts feel genuinely prioritised. JD Sports, Intersport, Snipes, and Zalando are all key. The risk: Nike returns to wholesale on the old terms. The opportunity: build a partnership model that no challenger can replicate.
Scope: Commercial operations, account strategy, planning
Opportunity 04
Member Data — ~160M Active Members Is a Large Asset Still Under-Monetised in EMEA
Nike reports approximately 160 million active members across its ecosystem (Nike.com, SNKRS, NRC, NTC apps) — generating extensive data on consumer behaviour, training habits, and purchase intent. Source: The Current — Nike Membership Programme Overview. The strategic view — framed as opinion — is that this engagement base is under-monetised in EMEA relative to what the underlying data asset would support across retail, replenishment and personalisation.
Scope: Digital operations, data strategy, commercial planning
Opportunity 05
Supply Chain Speed — From 18-Month Lead Times to Trend-Responsive Operations
Nike's EMEA supply chain was built for scale, not speed. 18-month product lead times meant Nike missed the "quiet luxury sportswear" trend, the trail running wave, and the padel explosion. On Running went from concept to shelf in under 9 months on several key launches. Nike needs a two-speed supply chain: core replenishment at scale, and a fast-lane process for trend-responsive product — structured around EMEA consumer signals.
Scope: Supply chain transformation, operations planning
Opportunity 06
Sustainability Credibility — Move to Zero Needs an EMEA Execution Plan
Nike's Move to Zero programme has genuine ambition: 100% renewable energy, zero carbon, zero waste across the supply chain. But in EMEA, where Gen Z consumers rank sustainability 2nd in brand selection criteria, Nike consistently underperforms versus On, Adidas, and Patagonia in sustainable perception surveys. The credentials exist; the consumer communication and in-store execution do not.
Scope: Brand operations, sustainability programme management
Opportunity 07
EMEA DTC Consistency — Close the Gap Between US Digital Experience and EMEA Reality
Nike.com in the US and Nike.com in Germany, Poland, or the Netherlands are materially different experiences — in personalisation, local content, fulfilment speed, and returns handling. The Hilversum team is responsible for EMEA digital operations, but resource allocation and platform prioritisation have been inconsistent. A structured EMEA DTC maturity uplift — market by market — is one of the highest-ROI programmes available.
Scope: Digital operations, e-commerce transformation, programme management
Opportunity 08
Operating Model for the Recovery — Hilversum Needs a Leaner, Faster Commercial Engine
The post-restructuring Nike EMEA organisation needs a commercial operating model built for the Hill era — not the Donahoe era. That means cleaner accountability between DTC and wholesale, faster commercial decision cycles, integrated planning across finance, supply chain, and commercial, and a ways-of-working reset for a team that has been through two years of uncertainty and headcount reduction.
Scope: Operating model design, change management, commercial planning
Opportunity 09 · EU Regulation
The EU ESPR Ban on Destruction of Unsold Apparel and Footwear — Effective 19 July 2026 for Large Enterprises
The EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) introduces a ban on the destruction of unsold apparel, clothing accessories and footwear that applies from 19 July 2026 for large enterprises, and from 19 July 2030 for medium-sized enterprises; micro and small enterprises are exempted. Source: Freshfields — ESPR unsold consumer products rules · Linklaters — Commission adopts final ESPR acts (Feb 2026). The planning implication — framed as opinion — is structural: demand forecasting must become more accurate, seasonal buy quantities tighter, and recommerce / circularity programmes must scale from pilot to mainstream. Brands with better planning infrastructure gain both a compliance advantage and a cost advantage.
Scope: Demand planning, inventory strategy, circularity programme, compliance
Opportunity 10 · Omni-channel
Omni-channel Planning — Nike Has the Infrastructure, Not the Execution Consistency
Nike scores Advanced on every omni-channel capability — online stock visibility, click & collect, ship-from-store, unified loyalty. But "capability exists" is not the same as "executed consistently across EMEA." In the Netherlands, Germany, and CEE markets, Nike's omni-channel experience materially underperforms the UK and US. Store-level stock visibility is unreliable. Click & collect fulfilment times vary by 3–5x across markets. Loyalty redemption is inconsistent. The capability investment has been made — the gap is in operational planning, process design, and commercial integration across wholesale and DTC channels. A structured EMEA omni-channel planning programme — covering inventory allocation logic, fulfilment routing, and partner integration — would convert existing infrastructure into a genuine competitive differentiator.
Scope: Omni-channel planning, retail operations, commercial integration

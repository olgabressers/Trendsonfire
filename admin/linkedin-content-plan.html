The Furniture Trap. That's the thought that stayed with me reading about Fonq filing for bankruptcy last week. After 18 years and half a million products, one of the Netherlands' most recognised online home retailers is gone. It would be easy to blame the housing market slowdown, or rising logistics costs. Both played a role. But they're not the real story. Made.com collapsed in 2022. Westwing has been restructuring for two years. Wayfair is still not profitable in Europe after years of investment. At some point the pattern becomes the point. The fundamentals of selling large furniture online are genuinely difficult. Return rates sit around 20–25%, and collecting a returned sofa costs €80–120 in logistics before you even think about restocking. Delivery for large items requires specialist carriers and two-person teams at €65–120 per drop. And the customer who just bought a wardrobe won't need another one for a decade. The unit economics are extremely hard to make work at any reasonable margin. The players growing in this category have something in common: they're not really solving these problems, they're structured to avoid them. IKEA's showrooms mean customers have already seen and decided before they order, which significantly reduces disappointment and returns. Bol.com operates as a marketplace — inventory risk and logistics sit with the seller, not the platform. Neither model is smarter. They're just built differently. The harder question is whether there is a model for online furniture that actually works at scale, or whether the category has permanently sorted itself into those who show you the product before you buy it and those who let someone else carry the risk. What do you think — I'd love to hear different perspectives in the comments, and I'll drop the full breakdown there too.

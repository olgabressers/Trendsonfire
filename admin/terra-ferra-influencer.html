01
Executive Summary
What the luxury influencer business model means for Terra Ferra
The research confirms the model works at scale, and the same research surfaces the infrastructure gap that defines the (emerging-)designer opportunity.
The direct answer to the question

Hard luxury houses disclose zero influencer-attributed revenue — the campaign model is engineered to drive desire to the boutique, not click to checkout. Affordable luxury houses use creators to drive customer acquisition to their own DTC sites and stores — none operate first-party in-platform checkout from creator posts. The conversion infrastructure that closes the sale on a creator's own audience is the missing layer for designers — and that is what Terra Ferra is built for.

01
The model is proven. Social commerce converts when infrastructure exists.
TikTok Shop reached $64B GMV — 60% creator-driven. Luxe Collective generated £2M selling pre-owned luxury through a livestream account. Coach acquired 4.6M customers through social. The conversion mechanic works at every scale. The question is not whether creators can sell — it is whether Terra Ferra's designers have the tools to do it.
02
The Dior model is inaccessible — but the underlying mechanic is not.
Luxury houses spend hundreds of millions on ambassador programmes that generate brand desirability, not direct checkout. (Emerging) designers cannot access that model. But they have something the Dior/Lisa campaign cannot replicate at small scale: a community that personally trusts them. That trust is the conversion driver. Terra Ferra's value is making it transactable.
03
Designers will not pay before they have earned.
Designers turning down a monthly fee before earning a single dollar from the platform is a familiar pattern in two-sided markets. Across PacSun, Coach, TikTok Shop and Luxe Collective, the same rule applies — sales come first, then the supply side commits. The order matters: first earning, then fee. Delivering that first earning is the operational unlock for the platform.

The luxury industry's deliberate choice to keep checkout out of influencer campaigns — $629M in Paris Fashion Week EMV, $1.3B at the Met Gala, €2.1B in Richemont's communication budget, none of it connected to a transaction layer — has produced an unserved market: designers with engaged audiences, no commercial infrastructure, and no platform built for their transaction. That is the unserved market this category needs to win.

(Emerging) designers have audiences they trust, and the conversion mechanic is proven at every scale, yet no platform serves this segment with the right infrastructure. The opportunity is to build the platform that closes the transaction — and Terra Ferra is positioned to be that platform.

The Data
What luxury brands get from influencer selling
The Question
What do luxury brands and affordable luxury brands actually get from selling through influencers — and what does that mean for the (emerging) designer market?
Section summary

Hard luxury (Cartier, Bulgari, Tiffany, Dior) routes influence to brand desirability — not checkout. Affordable luxury (Coach, Burberry, Hugo Boss, Tory Burch, Kate Spade, Ralph Lauren) uses creators to drive customer acquisition to their own DTC sites and stores — none of these brands operate first-party in-platform checkout from creator posts; the creator drives traffic, the brand's own site closes the sale. Both strategies work; neither is accessible at (emerging-)designer scale.

Luxury houses publishing influencer revenue. LVMH, Kering, Richemont, Hermès, Chanel — none disclose influencer-attributed sales as a separate line item in their published reports.

LVMH, Kering, Richemont, Hermès, Chanel route influence into the boutique — the influencer builds desire, the store closes the sale. The communication and brand-support budgets at the top of the industry are enormous, with no influencer-attributed sales line item disclosed by any house:

  • LVMH — marketing and selling expenses €29.9B in 2025 (37% of €80.8B revenue); advertising and promotion specifically reported at 11.5% of revenue in 2024.
  • Richemont — communication expenses €2,093M in FY25 (9.8% of sales).
  • Hermès — communication expenses €620M in 2025 (3.9% of €16.0B revenue).
  • Chanel — brand-support activities $2.45B in 2024 (around 13.1% of $18.7B revenue). Most recent published; FY25 publishes in May 2026.
  • Kering — does not break out communication or advertising as a separate line item in 2025 results. The closest disclosure is "other recurring operating expenses" of €6.25B (around 42.5% of €14.7B revenue), which bundles communication with store operations and distribution.

These houses have no interest in a checkout button next to their campaigns — it undermines the scarcity mechanics that justify the price point.

The one market where luxury social conversion has been measured is China: McKinsey documents around 30% purchase conversion via luxury influencer livestreaming — up to 10× conventional e-commerce — enabled by Douyin's integrated social commerce infrastructure. (McKinsey & Company) Western luxury has replicated none of this deliberately. The consumer converts when the infrastructure exists. The infrastructure choice belongs to the brand.

Platform Context — What Just Changed
Instagram relaunched creator affiliates in March 2026, after killing the first version four years earlier
Instagram's first native affiliate launched June 2021 in a closed US beta and shut down in August 2022 — creators inside the program could not tag products outside it, which shrank their reach. Between September 2022 and March 2026, Instagram had no native affiliate product while TikTok Shop, LTK, ShopMy and YouTube Shopping built creator-affiliate scale. The March 2026 relaunch is open: creators can paste any affiliate URL (LTK, ShopMy) and the transaction completes on the merchant's site. The key fact: 60% of TikTok Shop's $64B GMV is creator-driven — across all seller sizes, not just large brands. The conversion mechanic works at every scale. What (emerging) designers are missing is not an audience or a trust relationship with their followers. They are missing the commercial infrastructure that turns that trust into a transaction.

Pre-owned resellers proved that luxury audiences convert on social when infrastructure exists — Luxe Collective £2M on TikTok Shop, US authenticated resellers $30,000+ in a single livestream. Those operators sell other brands' products to audiences they rented through the platform. A designer with 15K engaged followers has something structurally different: an audience that already trusts them personally, and original products that cannot be bought anywhere else. The conversion opportunity is, if anything, stronger. The missing piece is identical: a checkout layer that sits between the creator and the fan.

05
The Infrastructure Gap
Designers have the audience. No platform gives them the tools.
Section summary

Every platform that exists for creator commerce was built for a different problem. Spring (merchandise), Patreon (subscriptions), Kickstarter (one-off crowdfunding), Shopify (DTC storefront with no audience) — none combines pre-sales, fan community, and revenue sharing for designers selling original collections. That is why no benchmark exists for "what a designer with 15K followers earns per collection launch." PacSun showed a 5,000-follower creator can drive 11,000 units in 48 hours when the infrastructure exists. The infrastructure is the gap.

Spring (formerly Teespring)
Creator Merchandise Platform
Allows creators to sell branded merchandise — t-shirts, hoodies, mugs, prints — without holding inventory. Prints on demand. Designed for social media creators monetising their personal brand, not for fashion designers pre-selling limited collections.
Missing for Terra Ferra designers
No pre-sale mechanism. No limited-edition scarcity model. No fan revenue sharing. No support for original fashion design — only merchandise printed on stock blanks.
Patreon
Creator Subscription Platform
Subscription-based membership model where fans pay monthly for exclusive access — content, behind-the-scenes, early posts. Primarily used by writers, podcasters, musicians, and artists. Revenue is recurring subscription income, not product sales.
Missing for Terra Ferra designers
Not a commerce platform. No product checkout. No pre-sale model. Fans pay for access, not for a garment. Fan revenue sharing does not exist — the designer earns from subscriptions, not from fan-driven sales.
Kickstarter
Crowdfunding Platform
One-time crowdfunding campaigns where creators pitch a product and backers pledge money before production. Fashion has one of the lowest success rates on the platform — fewer than 30% of completed fashion projects reach their funding goal. Source: CrowdCrux / Kickstarter, 2024.
Missing for Terra Ferra designers
One-time, not recurring. No ongoing fan relationship. No community commerce model. No revenue sharing with fans who promote the campaign. Requires significant campaign setup for each collection.
Shopify
Independent E-commerce
Full DTC storefront for any product. The most powerful commerce tool available to a small brand. Used by thousands of (emerging) designers to sell their collections directly. Requires the designer to handle all customer acquisition, marketing, fulfilment, and returns independently.
Missing for Terra Ferra designers
No built-in audience. No community. No fan revenue sharing to incentivise fans to promote. The designer builds a store — but still has to bring all the customers themselves. The audience-to-commerce link is not built in.

Why the benchmark data doesn't exist: there is no public data on "what a designer with 15K followers earns per collection launch" because no platform has served this market at scale and measured it. The data gap is not a research failure — it is the market gap. When a platform solves this problem and starts tracking it, that data will exist. Until then, the absence of the number is the evidence of the opportunity.

Influencer Selling in Luxury — What the Data Actually Shows

Prepared April 2026 · Sources: Launchmetrics, Lefty, WeArisma, Momentum Works, eMarketer, TikTok Newsroom, Richemont FY25

Luxury brands are the world's biggest investors in influencer marketing. They are also the only segment that has not converted that investment into a direct sales channel — and that is a deliberate choice, not a missed opportunity.

The mechanics are well established. Dior generates $84M in earned media value from a single Paris fashion week. Cartier's ambassador Jisoo drove 45% of the brand's entire annual influencer impact from 12 posts. At the 2025 awards season, Tiffany generated $24M in media value; Cartier $17M; Bulgari $12M. The numbers are large and the model is proven — influencers move luxury brand perception at scale.

What they do not do is sell. Not a single major luxury house — LVMH, Kering, Richemont — operates a first-party storefront on TikTok Shop or Instagram Shopping. The channel exists. The audience is there: 70% of TikTok's luxury audience has spent over £1,000 on a single fashion item, and 15% say they purchased luxury directly after platform exposure. Luxury brands are absent by choice. Their model is influence-to-boutique, not influence-to-checkout.

Meanwhile social commerce has become a serious retail channel. TikTok Shop reached $64B in global GMV in 2025, growing 94% year-on-year, with 60% of that driven by creators. The US social commerce market is $87B. Instagram generated $43B globally. The commerce infrastructure is built. The luxury sector is watching from outside.

The only luxury selling through TikTok Shop is pre-owned — authenticated resellers moving Hermès, Chanel, Cartier, and Louis Vuitton. Luxe Collective (UK) reached £2M in TikTok Shop sales within months of launching on the platform. The demand is there. The brands are not.

For affordable luxury — Burberry, Ralph Lauren, Coach — the picture is different. These brands are active on social platforms, capturing Gen Z at scale. Coach added 4.6 million new North American customers in FY25, roughly 70% Gen Z or Millennial. Burberry's fashion week EMV grew 83% year-on-year. They use social to sell, not just to brand.

The Answer

Luxury brands (hard luxury and designer houses) use influencers exclusively for brand building — not direct sales. There is no published revenue figure because there is no direct influencer sales channel. The impact is measured in Media Impact Value and Earned Media Value, not in transactions.

Affordable luxury brands use social to sell. They have measurable customer acquisition and conversion from social platforms, though few publish exact revenue figures by channel.

The gap between influence and transaction in luxury is deliberate. It is also the structural opportunity: the consumer is ready to buy through creators. The brands are not ready to let them.

On the data

MIV (Media Impact Value) — Launchmetrics proprietary metric. Measures content quality, source authority and reach. Industry standard for luxury. It is not sales revenue.

EMV (Earned Media Value) — used by Lefty and WeArisma. Engagement-based with platform multipliers. Not directly comparable to MIV.

No luxury house publishes influencer-attributed sales. Richemont's FY25 total communication expenses were ~€2.0B (9.7% of sales) with no channel breakdown. LVMH and Kering report identically. The absence of data is structural — attribution would require accepting that a creator, not the boutique, closed the sale.
